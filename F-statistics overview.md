
> [!Theory box]
> ##### Wright's F-statistics (fixation indices)
> **I** - Individual;
>
> **S** - Subpopulation;
>
> **T** - Total population.
>
> In the subpopulation, let the frequency of allele $A_1$ be $p_S$, such that the expected heterozygosity under random maiting is $H_S=2p_S(1-p_S)=2p_Sq_S$. 
>
> The same is true for the total population $H_T=2p_T(1-p_T)=2p_Tq_T$.
>
> And $H_I=f_{12}$ is the fraction of heterozygous individuals.
>
> Then inbreeding coefficients:
>
> $F_{IS}=1-\frac{H_I}{H_S}=1-\frac{f_{12}}{2p_Sq_S}$
>
> $F_{IT}=1-\frac{H_I}{H_T}=1-\frac{f_{12}}{2p_Tq_T}$
>
> $F_{ST}=1-\frac{H_S}{H_T}=1-\frac{2p_Sq_S}{2p_Tq_T}$
> 
> If summary across multiple subpopulations:
> 
> $\overline{F_{ST}}=1-\frac{\overline{H_{S}}}{H_T}$,
> 
> where $\overline{H_{S}}=\frac{1}{K}\sum_{i=1}^K{H_S^i}$, 
> 
> and $H_S^i=2p_iq_i$ is an expected heterozygosity in subpopulation $i$
> 
> $\overline{H_{S}}\leq H_T \implies \overline{F_{ST}}\geq 0$ and $\overline{F_{IS}}\leq \overline{F_{IT}}$
> 
> The latter is called Wahlund effect

If  $\overline{H_{S}} > H_T$ then $F_{ST} < 0$ , which raises a question whether it is possible to break down the population into several distinct subpopulations
